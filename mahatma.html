Unknown Doings of Mahatma

Published on May 18, 2023

Section 1 : Helping British in WW1

Mahatma Gandhi, often hailed as the father of the Indian independence movement,and apparently was an extraordinary figure whose philosophy was to promote idea of "non-violence". While his unwavering commitment to peaceful resistance is well-known, there are lesser-known aspects of Gandhi's life that shed light on the complexities of his beliefs and actions. In this blog post, we will explore how Gandhi, despite advocating for non-violence, supported the British Empire in recruiting Indian soldiers during World War I and World War II, ultimately leading to the loss of Indian lives.


World War 1

When World War I broke out in 1914, Britain called upon its colonies, including India, for support. Gandhi, who believed that India's support would grant it political leverage in the future, launched a recruitment campaign to encourage Indians to join the British army. Around 1.5 million Indian soldiers enlisted during the war, and tragically, more than 74,000 lost their lives.


World War 2

During World War II, Gandhi once again urged Indians to support the British war effort, even as the Indian National Congress adopted a stance of non-cooperation with the colonial administration. Gandhi believed that India's active involvement in the war would strengthen its case for independence after the conflict ended. Over two million Indian soldiers joined the British forces during World War II, with a staggering death toll of approximately 87,000.


Section 2 : Rigging Elections in favour of Nehru


1937: Unity over Victory

In the 1937 Congress presidential election, Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, a prominent leader and a close associate of Gandhi, emerged as the front-runner against Nehru. Despite Patel's popularity and overwhelming support, Gandhi expressed his unwavering trust in Nehru, endorsing him as the future leader of the Congress. Gandhi's decision aimed to promote unity within the party and prevent potential divisions that could undermine the freedom struggle.


1946: Bridging Divisions

The 1946 Congress presidential election witnessed a contest between Nehru and Pattabhi Sitaramayya. Although Sitaramayya was an influential leader and enjoyed the backing of various factions, including Gandhi's close associates, Gandhi firmly stood by Nehru. Gandhi believed that Nehru's inclusive and secular vision for an independent India aligned more closely with his own principles. Gandhi's endorsement of Nehru once again emphasized the need for unity and his belief in Nehru's ability to unite a diverse nation.


1947: The Dawn of Independence

As India stood on the cusp of independence, the Congress presidency assumed even greater significance. In the election held in 1947, Sardar Patel emerged as the favorite candidate, gaining support from influential leaders within the party. However, Gandhi, without any reservations, reiterated his support for Nehru, recognizing his ability to provide visionary leadership during the crucial phase of nation-building. Gandhi's endorsement reaffirmed his commitment to a united India and his belief in Nehru's capabilities.
